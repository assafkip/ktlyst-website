## ERA 1: THE ANALYST (2023) - Trust & Safety, Raw Voice

These are the earliest and most authentic samples. The voice here is: investigative, thorough, direct, systems-thinking, no polish. This is "most Assaf."

### Sample 1: Investigative Opening (Pig Butchering Analysis, Jun 2023)

> I wanted to do a deep analysis on how a pig butchering scam works. All I had to do was wait for a Linkedin message from a suspicious looking account. That did not take long. unfortunately, by the time I got to writing this article, that account had already blocked me which made me lose my interactions with them. Despite that, I can share the high level indicators of such an account and the information about the following conversation.

**Voice markers:** Casual setup ("All I had to do was wait"), lowercase "unfortunately" mid-sentence (ESL natural flow), practical framing ("I can share"), no drama - just walks you through it.

### Sample 2: Analytical Breakdown (Pig Butchering, scam mechanics)

> I utilized AI bots such as Bard, Claude and ChatGPT to run analysis on the conversation Tina and I had. Interesting enough, all 3 models were not able to identify any agenda within the conversation.
>
> This is very telling. To the uninformed victim, the conversation I had with Tina see to be totally benign. This is despite her clear efforts to get me to invest in her fraudulent scheme. This speaks to the sophistication of the operation as it skirts the boundaries of what we are used to with romance scams where the attacker is clearly going after money.

**Voice markers:** Tests things himself (doesn't theorize), "This is very telling" as a transition, explains what the finding MEANS not just what happened. Slight ESL grammar ("see to be" instead of "seems to be") - natural, not polished.

### Sample 3: Attacker Taxonomy (Whac-a-Mole Metrics, 2023)

> The opportunists/novice: Low sophistication actors using a limited number of accounts to make as much malicious impact as possible in a short amount of time. These actors run small scale operations and other limited scope attacks. They create their own accounts and handle their own infrastructure.
>
> The small/medium/up-and-comer organization: Medium sophistication actor group working in sync. They spend money to make money, creating or buying fake and compromised accounts in bulk, as well as collaborating with other groups and offenders using an array of infrastructure and attack surfaces.
>
> The large organization/criminal enterprise: Highly sophisticated actor group working across multiple online and offline abuse types. This type of organization could employ smaller malicious organizations to maintain a flow of fake and compromised accounts.

**Voice markers:** Classification instinct - Assaf naturally creates taxonomies. Tiered from simple to complex. Practical language ("spend money to make money"). This is the analyst brain at work.

### Sample 4: Systems Critique (Whac-a-Mole Metrics)

> Our measure of success is in preventing abuse. Essentially we are tasked with measuring what didn't happen. We need metrics to justify budgets, headcount, measure progress, efficiency and effectiveness. The need for measurement creates what I call the "whac-a-mole metric". In our effort to measure our work, we rely on the most readily available numbers. These tend to be mostly enforcement numbers, such as the number of accounts or entities removed, disabled, or restricted by our efforts.
>
> The core perception of this metric can be boiled down to a single principal: 1,000 accounts removed > 100 accounts removed.
>
> I argue that this type of metric creates a false narrative.

**Voice markers:** Names the problem ("whac-a-mole metric"), distills to a simple equation, then calls it out directly. "I argue" - takes a position. The naming instinct is core to Assaf's voice.

### Sample 5: Attacker-Defender Asymmetry (Whac-a-Mole Metrics)

> The goal of the adversarial actor is to get past our defenses. Once they are through, the actors are able to leverage their new posture to generate more significant impact. In the same way that we, as defenders, look to collect data to understand the attackers, so do the attackers collect data about us in order to make their attacks more successful.
>
> A popular and effective way of testing defenses is to use "throw away" or "cannon fodder" accounts. Since generating or buying fake and compromised accounts could be done at relatively low cost, adversaries have the ability to hit our defenses with hundreds and thousands of cheap accounts. The enforcement actions we apply to these accounts provide the adversaries with critical information, exposing our defense postures as well as their gaps and weaknesses.
>
> When we count the number of assets which have been subject to enforcement, we equate enforcement numbers to the scale of abuse perpetrated and our level of success curbing this abuse. These are false equivalencies.

**Voice markers:** Flips perspective to attacker's POV. "These are false equivalencies" - declarative, no hedging. Shows the structural flaw, not just the symptom.

### Sample 6: Proposed Framework (Whac-a-Mole Metrics)

> I suggest that instead of considering enforcement actions as a metric, we move to measuring our influence as an organization and a team. Influence comes in many shapes and sizes. What type of influence you will measure depends, among others, on the type of business your trust and safety organization resides in and the business goals. Once we have an understanding of the type of attacker/s we are dealing with, we can measure the holistic outcomes of mitigating or disrupting them.

**Voice markers:** "I suggest" - proposes, doesn't demand. Acknowledges context-dependence. Practical, not prescriptive. Then follows with a concrete framework (reduction metrics, appeal rates, network impact, product impact).

### Sample 7: CIB Experiment (Trump/DeSantis AI Article, 2023)

> Listening to an episode of Trust in Tech with Hallie J. Stern got my juices flowing about Coordinated Inauthentic Behavior (CIB) for profit. A subject I was deeply involved with during the 2020 US election. Back then we had bad actors plagiarizing real articles, scraping legitimate (and less legitimate) news sources to create their click bait, ad farm baiting stories.
>
> Times have changed. Now we have AI. I wanted to see if I can create a totally outlandish click bait article using AI. I first went to OpenAI - kudos to them, ChatGPT stopped me at every turn. So I turned to another one. lo and behold - a crazy article was born. Tell me you wouldn't click this headline. I would!

**Voice markers:** Triggered by real content consumption (podcast), references own operational experience (2020 election CIB), then TESTS IT HIMSELF. "Tell me you wouldn't click this headline. I would!" - direct, playful, honest.

### Sample 8: Military Story (Relationships Matter, 2023)

> One scorching summer day, during a live-fire exercise, I finally had the chance to showcase the power of artillery to my fellow paratroopers. Amidst the chaos and intensity of the exercise, my comms sergeant and I tirelessly coordinated communication and unleashed rounds on unsuspecting (staged) targets. It was an exhilarating experience that left us exhausted but fulfilled.
>
> Halfway through our trek, the exhaustion and dehydration took their toll on our bodies. With delirium setting in, we caught sight of the road, a glimmer of hope in our dire situation. In a stroke of luck, we spotted our buddies in the ambulance.

**Voice markers:** Storytelling mode - more descriptive than analytical writing. Real military detail (artillery liaison, comms sergeant, paratroopers). This voice appears when Assaf tells personal stories vs. analytical pieces. More adjectives, more scene-setting.

### Sample 9: Layoff Raw Processing (Week 1, 2023)

> 2 months ago, I experienced a layoff from Meta, triggering a mix of emotions. Reflecting on my time with the eCrime team, I felt both sadness and excitement for what lies ahead.

**Voice markers:** This whole piece uses emojis, exclamation marks, and a more vulnerable/raw tone. It's a processing piece, not an analytical one. Shows Assaf's range - he CAN be personal and emotional, but his default analytical mode is what appears in professional content.

## ERA 2: THE CONSULTANT (Sep 2025) - T&S Frameworks, Voice Sharpening

### Sample 10: Scale as Teacher (Scale Changes Everything, Sep 2025)

> The biggest shift in my career didn't come from the logos on my resume.
> It came from scale and its implications.
>
> When you work at very large scale in Trust & Safety or fraud, abuse looks different.
> You stop thinking in one-offs.
>
> One-off rules
> Block lists
> Allow lists
>
> They feel like solutions when you're small. But at scale, there are no solutions to abuse.

**Voice markers:** Anti-status opening ("not from the logos"). Ultra-short lines. List format for emphasis. "At scale, there are no solutions to abuse" - blunt thesis statement. This is peak distilled Assaf.

### Sample 11: Scale Implications (Scale Changes Everything)

> At scale, you see how a single tactic ripples into millions in losses.
> You see adversaries adapt faster than policies, models, or leadership priorities.
> You see the same detection logic rebuilt again and again because institutional knowledge doesn't travel.
>
> At scale, abuse doesn't look like isolated incidents.
> It looks like systems failing to carry lessons forward.
> It looks like defenders still fighting case by case while attackers run structured experiments in parallel.
>
> That's why I balk at block lists.
> That's why I cringe at one-off rules.
> They don't survive scale.

**Voice markers:** Anaphora ("At scale, you see... You see... You see..."). Building pattern. "institutional knowledge doesn't travel" - this is the KTLYST thesis in one sentence, before KTLYST existed. Emotional verbs: "balk," "cringe" - controlled frustration.

### Sample 12: Scale Closing (Scale Changes Everything)

> Scale changes everything.
> It changed me.
>
> It's why I no longer settle for rules that feel good in the moment but don't hold up under pressure.
> Because safety debt compounds. And the cost of missing the pattern isn't a bad ticket, it's broken trust, financial loss, and regulators asking questions you can't answer.

**Voice markers:** Two-word paragraph. Personal admission ("It changed me"). "Safety debt compounds" - the compounding metaphor that carries into KTLYST. Ends with consequences, not inspiration.

## ERA 3: THE BRIDGE (Late 2025) - From T&S to Security

### Sample 13: Signal Sharing Critique (Signal Sharing Mirage, 2025)

> Signal sharing is broken. Not because people do not care, but because most of what passes for sharing is compliance theater.
>
> Coalitions issue PDFs. Agencies send advisories. Companies announce partnerships. It looks like collaboration. In practice, it is a checkbox.
>
> The signals being passed around are not designed to help users or take down bad actors. They are designed to show regulators, boards, and the press that something is being done.

**Voice markers:** Opens with a flat declaration. "Compliance theater" - naming the dysfunction. Three short sentences building the case. "It is a checkbox" - devastating simplicity. This is the sharpest Assaf voice.

### Sample 14: Ingestion Problem (Signal Sharing Mirage)

> Most Trust and Safety teams are not set up to handle large volumes of external indicators. What lands in inboxes are lists of IPs, domains, or accounts. There are rarely pipelines to validate, prioritize, or translate those into local defenses.
>
> Even teams with strong technical infrastructure struggle because raw indicators lack context. A thousand IPs with no pattern or timing data are not actionable. Without understanding how the abuse worked, when it happened, and which seams were exploited, the signals are little more than noise.
>
> So they sit in spreadsheets or ticket queues. They never become detection rules.

**Voice markers:** Specific operational details (IPs, domains, spreadsheets, ticket queues). "They never become detection rules" - short sentence that drops like a verdict. Practical, not theoretical.

### Sample 15: Attacker Feedback Loops (Signal Sharing Mirage)

> Abuse actors treat every enforcement as a data point. A block tells them where the wall is. An approval shows them a path. They share those results freely across forums and networks.
>
> Defenders do the opposite. We bury incidents in privilege, polish reports for compliance, and call it sharing. The imbalance is obvious. Attackers have operational feedback loops. We have paperwork.

**Voice markers:** Perfect contrast structure. "Attackers have operational feedback loops. We have paperwork." - this is signature Assaf. Devastating two-sentence comparison.

### Sample 16: The Cost (Signal Sharing Mirage, closing)

> I have been in too many rooms where the question was, "Why did nobody warn us?" The truth is someone probably saw the same pattern weeks earlier. But the signal stayed locked up because the sharing model was not designed for operations. It was designed to show effort.
>
> As long as that is the case, abusers will keep moving faster. And users will keep paying the cost.

**Voice markers:** "I have been in too many rooms" - the witness. "It was designed to show effort" - reframe. Ends with consequences for real people (users), not abstract system failure.

## ERA 4: THE FOUNDER (Dec 2025 - 2026) - KTLYST, Refined Voice

### Sample 17: LinkedIn Post (60 Muscles, Mar 2026)

> Every enterprise I've worked inside had 60+ security tools. Threat intel, detection, SOC, IR, red team, GRC - each with their own stack, their own tribal knowledge, their own version of reality.
>
> At Google, I watched four teams fight the same scam operation across ads, search, YouTube, and safe browsing. None of them knew it. The ads team would take down a warhead. The attacker changed one thing and came back, because nobody touched the infrastructure.
>
> That's 60 muscles with no nervous system.

**Voice markers:** Google scar, "their own version of reality," naming the metaphor. This is the most polished version of the voice - still direct, but tighter.

### Sample 18: Medium Article Opening (60 Muscles, Mar 2026)

> Every enterprise I've worked inside had 60+ security tools. Threat intel platforms. SIEMs. SOAR playbooks. EDR agents. Red team frameworks. GRC dashboards. Each one does its job. Each one is, on its own, good enough.
>
> The problem isn't the tools. It's what doesn't exist between them.

### Sample 19: Medium Article Closing (60 Muscles, Mar 2026)

> That's the real question. Not "do we have enough tools?" but "does what we learn actually change what we do?"
>
> If you can't answer that - and almost nobody can - the problem isn't your tools. It's the space between them.

## Signature Patterns (Collected Across All Eras)

### "X isn't Y. It's Z."
- "That's not documentation. It's graffiti."
- "AI isn't a feature. It's a system."
- "The missing layer isn't another tool. It's the nervous system."
- "That's not a tools problem. That's an organizational nervous system problem."
- "I argue that this type of metric creates a false narrative."

### "X does A. It doesn't do B."
- "SIEMs process events. They don't process learning."
- "SOAR automates responses inside one silo. It doesn't govern what flows between silos."
- "Attackers have operational feedback loops. We have paperwork."

### Naming the dysfunction
- "whac-a-mole metric"
- "compliance theater"
- "safety debt"
- "shelfware"
- "folklore" (vs. record)
- "false equivalencies"

### The uncomfortable question
- "When the CISO asks 'What did our threat intel program actually prevent?' Silence."
- "Does what we learn actually change what we do?"
- "How do we know if we are actually reducing the level of abuse on the platform?"
- "Why did nobody warn us?"

### The witness line
- "I have been in too many rooms where..."
- "Four teams fought the same threat actor at Google. None of them knew."
- "I wanted to see if I can..."
- "At scale, you see..."
- **ANTI-PATTERN:** Never template this as "At [Company], I watched/saw..." or "I watched this play out at [Company]." Vary the entry. Drop into the scene. Let the company name land naturally inside the action, not as a credential prefix.

### The blunt closer
- "Most organizations never get there."
- "They don't survive scale."
- "They never become detection rules."
- "And users will keep paying the cost."

## Anti-Patterns: Things Assaf Would NEVER Write

### From his OWN writing (the ASK Integrity launch post, Jun 2023):

> "I am thrilled to share the exciting news of my new venture"
> "honed my ability to connect cross-functional teams"
> "implementing cutting-edge mechanisms"
> "leveraging advanced techniques"
> "navigating the ever-evolving landscape"
> "fostering a foundation for future communication"
> "Lets join forces to create a safer digital world"

This post uses every banned word and every empty phrase. Assaf himself would recognize this is NOT his voice - it reads like an AI-written announcement. The analytical posts from the same period (Pig Butchering, Whac-a-Mole) are the real voice.

### Generic AI voice (never write like this):
- "In today's rapidly evolving threat landscape, organizations must..."
- "We're proud to announce our innovative new platform..."
- "As a passionate cybersecurity leader, I believe that..."
- "Excited to connect with fellow security professionals at..."
- "Just checking in to see if you had a chance to review..."
- "Hope this finds you well!"
- "Thoughts? Let me know in the comments!"

## Voice Evolution Summary

| Era | Period | Domain | Tone | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Analyst | 2023 | T&S, Fraud | Raw, investigative, thorough | Tests things himself, taxonomies, attacker POV |
| Consultant | Sep 2025 | T&S at scale | Distilled, declarative, short lines | "Scale changes everything" - thesis emerging |
| Bridge | Late 2025 | T&S -> Security | Sharp, blunt, systems critique | "Compliance theater," naming dysfunction |
| Founder | 2026 | Cybersecurity / SLCP | Polished but still direct, metaphor-rich | CNS metaphor, cross-industry proof, no pitch |

**The constant across all eras:** Practitioner authority, systems thinking, naming dysfunction, attacker-aware framing, direct sentences, no filler.

**What changed:** Domain vocabulary (T&S -> cybersecurity), metaphor sophistication (scale -> nervous system), audience (practitioners -> investors + practitioners).
